00 — The brief

What we're trying to accomplish

The official Bard notes ask for instruments to become central to the class rather than optional flourishes: more damage songs, more songs that genuinely require an instrument, and less reliance on permanent, set-and-forget charm. The hard part isn't adding songs — it's making the instrument in your hands matter without burying the class in math.

So this version is deliberately simple. Each instrument grants one clear, flat benefit that applies to every song you play — pick up a different instrument and you change how all your songs behave, not just one. There's no warm-up curve to read and no percentage that creeps; the only thing that stacks at all is a single Brass mechanic, and it's capped. Choosing an instrument is a real decision, and the numbers stay legible.

It's a fair bit less machinery than an earlier draft of this proposal — on purpose. The depth now lives in which instrument you commit to and when, not in a stat that ramps behind the scenes.

01 — The core loop

Cadence, and the game tick

A bard keeps a song alive by replaying it about every 3 seconds. The song's effect — damage, a heal, a tick of mana — lands on the world's 6‑second tick, the heartbeat that everything else in the world already runs on. Two casts per tick is the rhythm the whole class sits on.

The one change we'd recommend

Today a song's effect tends to count time from the moment you cast it. We'd recommend instead that bard effects resolve on the game tick — sampled on the shared heartbeat rather than on a private per-cast timer. It keeps every bard's effects breathing with the same pulse as the rest of the encounter, and it makes timing a decision instead of a quiet dice-roll on whether your cast happened to line up.

Not a hard requirement. Everything below works just as well if effects keep counting from each cast. Only the feel of the timing changes — anchoring to the shared heartbeat is the version we think reads cleanest.
02 — The lock-in

Attunement

Attunement isn't a number — it's a switch. Pick up an instrument and its benefit comes online after 3 casts; swap to a different instrument and it switches off, costing you those 3 casts to earn back.

That single rule is the whole anti‑toxic‑switching mechanic. Because the benefit takes a moment to lock in and resets on a swap, you can't bounce between instruments mid‑fight to grab the best buff for each individual song. And you have no reason to: the benefits below already apply to every song you play, so the instrument is a commitment for the fight, not a per‑song toggle. You twist songs freely; you just do it on one instrument.

# Attunement — a switch, not a number instrument benefit → ON after 3 casts swap instrument → OFF (re-earn the 3 casts)
03 — Four instruments, four benefits

The instruments

You hold one at a time, and each is a single flat benefit to all your songs. Two of them buy you reach — faster casts or longer songs — and two buy you magnitude — flat power now, or power that rewards commitment.

Windreach · timing

−0.5s cast time (3.0s → 2.5s). A tighter rotation, yes — but the real value is timing: slipping a charm or mez in, or re‑applying a song right after a break, before the next tick lands. A precision tool more than a breadth tool.

cast −0.5s3.0s → 2.5s
Stringreach · uptime

+6s duration (18s → 24s) — exactly one more tick of life on every song. Hold a wider twist without anything falling off, and keep effects reaching further across the heartbeat for multi‑target coverage.

duration +6s18s → 24s
Percussionmagnitude · now

+10% effect per cast. Two casts a tick means roughly +20% throughput, and it lands immediately on every song from the moment you're attuned. No ramp, no commitment — the reliable generalist that just makes everything hit harder.

effect +10% / cast~+20% / tick
Brassmagnitude · commitment

+5% effect per recast, building to +35%. This is resonance — and it's Brass's alone. It ramps slowly (seven recasts to cap) and resets if the song drops, so twisting barely moves it. It pays off only when you stay on one song, which is why Brass lives on single‑effect songs.

effect +5% / recastmax +35%resets on drop
Wind −0.5s cast (3.0s → 2.5s) String +6s duration (18s → 24s, one extra tick) Percussion +10% effect / cast (~+20% per 6s tick) Brass +5% effect / recast → max +35%, resets on drop # delivered value = base × (1 + the active instrument's bonus)
No instrument — singing only. The baseline: none of the above, just the bard's voice. Everything here is the upside of actually playing an instrument.
04 — Why Brass commits

Brass lives on single effects, not twisted DoTs

Running the numbers settles an old question. A multi‑DoT twist always out‑damages a single sustained DoT on one target — it isn't close. So a single‑song "dedication" is intentionally not a single‑target DPS path.

That's exactly why Brass's slow‑ramping +35% belongs on the things that are single‑effect by nature — a heal, a charm, a hate spike, a buff held on one target — rather than on twisted damage. Brass resonance resets the moment a song drops and barely climbs while you twist, so the system steers itself: commit to one effect and Brass rewards you; spread thin and it does almost nothing. The Serenade ability below is the tool built for exactly that committed moment.

05 — Two activated abilities

Chorus & Serenade

The instruments are passive. Two activated abilities let the bard spike on demand: one swells the whole performance, the other pours it into a single target.

ChorusActivated · overcharge

For 18 seconds, every song you play hits for +50% effect, regardless of instrument — then it's gone. It stacks additively: on a fully‑ramped Brass song (+35%) that's +85% for the window. The band‑swelling burst button — line it up with your biggest moment and let it ring.

# Chorus — activated, all songs, instrument-agnostic +50% effect to all songs, 18s, additive (Brass → +85%)
SerenadeActivated · single target

Activate Serenade and your next song becomes a 6‑second performance aimed at one target, applying once per second — six rapid ticks, in the spirit of how bandaging channels today. It's deliberately simple now: six hits of the song's effect on a single target, no extra scaling to track. Use it to dump a committed song into one enemy or ally.

# Serenade — activated, channels on one target next song → 6s channel, 1 tick / second (6 ticks, single target)

Together they compound: a Serenade fired inside a Chorus is six single‑target ticks at the boosted rate — the bard's single biggest moment. (Chorus is the one number we may still tune or drop; the additive +85% peak is the thing to watch.)

06 — The point of all of it

The decisions this creates

A short list of the choices a bard now gets to make:

Commitment

Ramp the heal

On Brass, hold the healing hymn and let resonance climb toward +35% before the tank gets hit — or pop Chorus for +50% right now. Foresight versus the panic button.

Focus fire

Serenade a target

Channel a committed song into one target as a six‑tick burst — and stack it under Chorus for the biggest single hit in the kit.

Reach

Twist wide or play precise

String for a wider rotation that never drops, Wind to land a charm or mez in the gap before the tick. Coverage versus control.

Read the fight

Pick the instrument

One instrument for the fight — drum for steady power, brass for a committed effect, wind or string for reach. The choice is up front, and switching costs you.

08 — Over to you

Open questions & an invitation

This is the pared‑down version: four flat instrument benefits, one stacking Brass mechanic, a simple lock‑in that discourages instrument‑hopping, and two activated abilities on top. No hidden curves, nothing that spirals.

The open question is whether even this is the right amount — whether Chorus earns its place, whether the numbers are tuned right, whether the lock‑in is the cleanest way to discourage swapping. Suggestions, holes, and "still too much" are all welcome. Read it, open the model, and tell me where it's wrong.
